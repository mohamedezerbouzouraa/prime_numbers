The infinite mystery of prime numbers

From Fermat's beautiful conjecture — shattered by Euler — to Wilson's elegant divisibility criterion and the deepest unsolved problems in mathematics.

Mohamed Ezer Bouzouraa Software & AI Engineer  · 

There is something quietly astonishing about the fact that buried within the ordinary sequence of counting numbers there exists a hidden skeleton — a sparse, irregular, yet perfectly lawful set of values from which every other integer is built. These are the primes: numbers divisible by nothing but one and themselves. They are, in a very precise sense, the atoms of arithmetic. And for centuries, mathematicians have sought formulas that generate, detect, or predict them — often with spectacular failure, and occasionally with profound success.

The story of prime-generating formulas is not a story of steady progress. It is a story of beautiful ideas that collapse under examination, of conjectures that endure for decades before a single counterexample destroys them, and of theorems so elegant they seem too good to be true — yet are rigorously true. Let us trace that story from its most celebrated ruins to its most durable monuments.

Fermat's conjecture and Euler's refutation

Pierre de Fermat — the seventeenth-century lawyer and amateur mathematician who arguably did more for number theory than any professional of his age — noticed a striking pattern in 1640. Consider the sequence of numbers obtained by raising 2 to a power that is itself a power of 2, and adding one:

Fermat Numbers Fn = 22n + 1 For n = 0, 1, 2, 3, 4, … producing F₀ = 3, F₁ = 5, F₂ = 17, F₃ = 257, F₄ = 65537

Each of these — 3, 5, 17, 257, 65537 — is prime. Fermat examined the first five values and, finding all of them prime, conjectured that every number of this form is prime. The conjecture was bold, the evidence compelling, and the pattern seductive.

"Fermat had the rare gift of formulating conjectures that were almost right — which is a more dangerous talent than being mostly wrong."

— Mathematical folklore

Then came Leonhard Euler. In 1732, nearly a century after Fermat's conjecture, the most prolific mathematician in history turned his attention to the next Fermat number: F₅ = 232 + 1 = 4,294,967,297. Euler discovered — through a combination of theoretical insight and systematic calculation — that this number factors as:

Euler's Refutation (1732) F₅ = 4,294,967,297 = 641 × 6,700,417 Euler proved 641 | F₅ by observing that 641 = 5⁴ + 2⁴ = 5 × 2⁷ + 1, then using modular arithmetic to show F₅ ≡ 0 (mod 641)

The conjecture was dead. What made Euler's proof so elegant was not brute computation but a theoretical argument. He showed that any prime factor of Fn (for n ≥ 2) must be of the form k · 2n+2 + 1 for some integer k — which dramatically restricts the candidates and explains why 641 works.

In the centuries since Euler, mathematicians have factored or proved composite every Fermat number they have fully investigated — and yet no new Fermat prime has ever been found beyond F₄ = 65,537. As of today, Fermat numbers F₅ through F₃₂ are all known to be composite. Beyond that, most are so astronomically large that even factoring them partially is a significant computational achievement.

Wilson's theorem — a perfect characterisation

Where Fermat's formula fails as a prime generator, Wilson's theorem succeeds as a prime detector — beautifully, definitively, and yet impractically. First stated by John Wilson in 1770 and proved by Joseph-Louis Lagrange the same year, it gives a necessary and sufficient condition for primality expressed entirely through factorials.

Wilson's Theorem (1770) p is prime ⟺ (p − 1)! ≡ −1 (mod p)

A natural number p > 1 is prime if and only if (p − 1)! + 1 is divisible by p. Equivalently, (p − 1)! leaves remainder p − 1 when divided by p.

The elegance is striking. For p = 5: (5−1)! = 24, and 24 + 1 = 25 = 5 × 5. For p = 7: 6! = 720, and 720 + 1 = 721 = 7 × 103. For p = 11: 10! = 3,628,800, and 3,628,800 + 1 = 3,628,801 = 11 × 329,891. It works perfectly — every time, for every prime.

Why it is true

The key insight is about modular inverses. In the integers modulo a prime p, every element from 1 to p−1 has a multiplicative inverse — an element it multiplies with to give 1. Most elements pair up with a distinct inverse: the product of each such pair contributes a factor of 1 to (p−1)!. The only elements that are their own inverse are those satisfying x² ≡ 1 (mod p), which (for prime p) means only x = 1 and x = p−1. So the entire product (p−1)! collapses to 1 × (p−1) ≡ −1 (mod p).

For composite numbers, the argument breaks: if p = ab with 1 < a, b < p, then both a and b appear in the product (p−1)! making it divisible by p, so (p−1)! ≡ 0, not −1, modulo p. The biconditional is tight.

Why it is impractical

To test whether a 300-digit number is prime using Wilson's theorem, one would need to compute the factorial of a 300-digit number — a quantity with roughly 10300 digits. No computer, past, present, or conceivable future, could perform this calculation. Wilson's theorem is mathematically perfect and computationally useless, which is precisely why number theorists love it and engineers ignore it.

Other prime-detecting and prime-generating formulas

The history of attempts to "formula-tise" the primes is long and largely humbling. But several formulas stand out — either for their theoretical importance, their surprising elegance, or their instructive failures.

Euler's remarkable quadratic: x² + x + 41

In 1772, Euler discovered that the polynomial x² + x + 41 produces prime values for every integer x from 0 to 39 — a run of forty consecutive primes. No other simple polynomial is known to match this streak.

Euler's Prime-Rich Polynomial f(x) = x² + x + 41 f(0)=41, f(1)=43, f(2)=47, f(3)=53, … f(39)=1601 — all prime. But f(40) = 1681 = 41² — composite.

The reason for this behaviour is connected to the theory of imaginary quadratic fields and class numbers — but the magic inevitably breaks. No polynomial with integer coefficients can produce only primes, since substituting x = p (where p is any prime the polynomial generates) makes the result divisible by p.

The Sieve of Eratosthenes

Not a formula in the algebraic sense, but an algorithm that has endured for over 2,000 years. To find all primes up to N: list every integer from 2 to N, then repeatedly cross out multiples of each prime you encounter. What remains is the complete set of primes up to N.

Sieve — complexity O(N log log N) Optimal for finding all primes up to N. Still used in practice — segmented versions handle billions efficiently.

Fermat's little theorem — the foundation of primality testing

Not to be confused with Fermat's Last Theorem or Fermat numbers, Fermat's Little Theorem is among the most practically useful results in all of number theory, and it underlies almost every modern primality test.

Fermat's Little Theorem If p is prime and gcd(a, p) = 1, then aᵖ⁻¹ ≡ 1 (mod p)

Equivalently: for any integer a not divisible by prime p, raising a to the power p − 1 and dividing by p always leaves remainder 1.

This gives a fast probabilistic primality test: pick a random a, compute ap−1 mod p (efficiently, using fast exponentiation), and check if the result is 1. If not, p is definitely composite. If yes, p is probably prime — though so-called Carmichael numbers can fool this test. The Miller–Rabin test, a refinement, is what modern systems actually use.

The Lucas–Lehmer test for Mersenne primes

A Mersenne prime is a prime of the form 2p − 1 where p itself is prime. The largest known primes are almost always Mersenne primes, found by the distributed computing project GIMPS. The Lucas–Lehmer test determines whether a Mersenne number is prime with remarkable efficiency.

Lucas–Lehmer Primality Test Define s₀ = 4, sₙ = sₙ₋₁² − 2 (mod 2ᵖ − 1). Then 2ᵖ − 1 is prime ⟺ s_{p−2} ≡ 0 (mod 2ᵖ − 1)

Requires only p − 2 squarings modulo a Mersenne number. The largest known prime (as of 2024) has over 41 million digits and was found by this method.

The prime counting function and its approximations

Let π(N) denote the number of primes ≤ N. The Prime Number Theorem establishes the asymptotic behaviour, but sharper approximations exist:

Prime Number Theorem & Refinements π(N) ~ N / ln(N) [PNT, Hadamard & de la Vallée Poussin, 1896] π(N) ~ Li(N) = ∫₂ᴺ dt / ln(t) [logarithmic integral, sharper] π(N) = Li(N) + O(√N · ln N) [assuming Riemann Hypothesis]

The logarithmic integral Li(N) outperforms N/ln(N) by a full order of magnitude. The Riemann Hypothesis, if proved, would give the tightest known error bound.

The n-th prime approximation

Inverting the prime counting function, we can approximate the n-th prime pn:

Approximation of the n-th prime pₙ ≈ n · ln(n) + n · ln(ln(n)) − n For n = 1000: p₁₀₀₀ = 7919. This formula gives ≈ 7909. Error: 0.13%

The Riemann hypothesis — primes in the complex plane

All of the approximations above carry an implicit error term — a gap between formula and reality. The deepest question in prime theory is: exactly how large can that gap be? Bernhard Riemann, in his single 1859 paper on the distribution of primes, connected this question to the zeros of a function of a complex variable.

The Riemann Zeta Function ζ(s) = Σₙ₌₁^∞ 1/nˢ = ∏ₚ (1 − p⁻ˢ)⁻¹ The product on the right (the Euler product) runs over all primes p — encoding the entire prime distribution in a single analytic function.

The Euler product formula — due to Euler himself, predating Riemann — already reveals the deep connection: ζ(s) can be written as a product over primes, meaning its analytic properties encode arithmetic facts about the primes. Riemann's insight was to study where ζ(s) = 0 in the complex plane. His conjecture, never proved, is that all non-trivial zeros lie on the critical line Re(s) = ½.

"If we had a proof of the Riemann Hypothesis, we would immediately know the distribution of primes with a precision no computation can match. The proof has eluded every mathematician who has lived since 1859."

Open conjectures — what we still do not know

Despite centuries of effort and the most powerful mathematical machinery ever assembled, the primes still withhold their deepest secrets. Several ancient conjectures remain unproved:

Goldbach's conjecture (1742) Every even integer n > 2 is the sum of two primes

Verified computationally up to 4 × 10¹⁸. No proof exists. Widely believed to be true.

Twin prime conjecture There are infinitely many pairs (p, p+2) both prime

Examples: (3,5), (5,7), (11,13), (17,19), (29,31)... Zhang (2013) proved infinitely many pairs separated by at most 246. The gap to 2 remains unbridged.

Are there infinitely many Fermat primes? Fn = 22n + 1 prime for infinitely many n?

Suspected answer: no. Only F₀ through F₄ are known prime. No proof of finiteness or infinitude exists.

Primes through the lens of AI

As a software and AI engineer, I find a particular resonance in the way prime numbers illuminate the limits of pattern recognition. Modern machine learning systems excel at identifying statistical regularities in data — yet the primes, while not random, are irregular enough that no neural network has discovered a closed-form expression for the n-th prime. The sequence passes standard tests of randomness in many respects, yet it is completely deterministic and structured.

Recent work has applied deep learning to questions in analytic number theory — notably DeepMind's collaboration with mathematicians to discover new connections in the statistics of L-functions. The results suggest AI may help illuminate relationships humans have missed, not by replacing proof but by generating hypotheses worth pursuing. Fermat generated hypotheses; Euler refuted them. That interplay — conjecture, test, refutation, refinement — is something machines are beginning to participate in meaningfully.

The primes remain the purest stress test for any reasoning system: simple to define, impossible to fully tame, and generous enough in their mysteries to keep mathematicians and engineers alike occupied for centuries yet to come.
